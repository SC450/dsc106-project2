Proposition: NYPD officers are more often cleared of allegations than found guilty, with incrimination rates remaining proportional over time.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Title and description: I made sure to use language that would be in support of the proposition. Using the phrase "no one is above the law" creates an engaging and eye-catching title, and I honestly gave a description of the plot in the description. I would rate this decision's earnestness as 1.5.
  • Annotations: I really wanted this visualization to agree with the proposition's point that both lines grow proportional to each other, so I emphasized the proportional growth with orange arrows, which show the scalar by which you can multiply the value at the "Action Taken" line to reach the value at the "No Action Taken" line. Additionally, I added text which restates that both complaint types accumulate proportionally to each other. I would rate this decision's earnestness as 2.
  • Color: I chose colors that would stand out from each other and be unique. The blue color for the "No Action Taken" line is a calm and neutral color, while the red color for the "Action Taken" line is a more aggressive color. I chose the arrows to be orange, as orange is distinguishable from both blue and red, and clearly emphasizes the proportionality between the two lines. I would rate this decision's earnestness as 2.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Grouping and aggregation: I created the "No Action Taken" and "Action Taken" categories by grouping similar values together for each category. For example, values in the "Action Taken" category include arrests of any kind, as well as summons of any kind. This makes the category of NYPD officers who have done wrongdoing look larger than it would be if I had separated the arrests and summons into two separate categories. I have also been intentionally vague in naming the "Action Taken" category, and I avoided using the words "arrests" or "summons issued" to make this plot have the same labels as the above persuasive plot. This forces the reader to read more closely to identify that the "Action Taken" category encompasses arrests and court summons, as described in the description of the plot. I would rate this decision's earnestness as -1.5.
  • Title and description: I made sure to use negative language in my title to persuade the reader that more recent years have seen a greater number of NYPD officers being punished for their actions. Using the phrase "modern generations of NYPD officers are failing themselves" catches the reader's attention by utilizing a negative connotation. I also stated in my description that the rate of complaints in which there is no action taken has "fallen behind", emphasizing the idea that NYPD officers have become more troublesome over time. I would rate this decision's earnestness as -1.
  • Annotations: I wanted to emphasize that modern generations of officers have been more problematic than officers of past years, so I annotated the plot with an orange line at the year 2005, which is when the "Action Taken" line overtakes the "No Action Taken" line. I also added an arrow and text annotation to make this point clear. The orange line serves a "divisor" between the older and newer years, and makes it easy to see that both lines have clearly not been growing proportionally to each other. I would rate this decision's earnestness as 1.5.

Final Reflection

Designing both visualizations was both easier and harder than I had expected. I had a clear vision of what I wanted both visualizations to argue for, but I encountered difficulty in finding deceptive techniques I could use in my plot against the proposition. I wanted to make both visualizations look very similar to each other so that it would not be immediately obvious to the reader what the deceptive technique was. This is why I used the same colors for each plot, as well as the same axes and labels. This makes the plots look very similar to each other, even though they are arguing for different sides of the proposition, forcing the reader to look more closely at the plots to identify the differences, which can be found detailed in the description of the plots. The labels "Action Taken" and "No Action Taken" are the same for both plots and are intentionally vague, as I wanted to use the same labels for both plots despite both plots conveying different meanings. "Action Taken" and "No Action Taken" are broad, general terms that still correctly describe the data, but do not give the reader the full picture of what the data is actually showing.

I was surprised by how different conclusions could be drawn depending on which columns of the data were shown, and how data was grouped. In my visualization for the proposition, I used the "board_disposition" column, grouping data based on whether or not values contain the string "substantiated". Similarly, in my visualization against the proposition, I used the "outcome_description" column, grouping data based on whether or not values contain the string "arrest" or "summons issued". After grouping the values for both columns, I had thought that "board_disposition" and "outcome_description" would have roughly the same distribution of "Action Taken" and "No Action Taken", as I thought substantiations would correspond to an equal amount of arrests and summons issued, and unsubstantiated complaints would correspond to an equal amount of no action taken. However, this is not the case, as shown by the two visualizations. This taught me that although two columns may seem similar, they can have completely different distributions of values.

After making my deceptive visualization, I realized that honesty and clarity are important when analyzing and visualizing data ethically. Using ambiguous labels in data visualizations can trick the reader into interpreting data from a warped perspective, biased depending on what the visualization is arguing for. I have also discovered that language plays a significant role in affecting how a visualization can be viewed, as language with strong connotations can easily sway the reader into agreeing with a visualization's argument. A bound that I would draw to distinguish acceptable persuasive choices vs. misleading ones is using unambiguous labels that accurately describe the data, while still using language that is engaging and eye-catching. Using broad terms can hide facts about the data from the reader, which starts treading into misleading territory. I think transparency in how data is grouped is also another important boundary between acceptable and misleading persuasive choices. Avoiding elaboration on how data is grouped and labeled can lead the reader to believe that a category of data is bigger or smaller than it actually is, and makes the visualization deceptive. I do not think that it is necessary for the reader to understand the fine details of the data grouping process, but the reader should be able to understand what kind of values fall into what category and for what reason.
